How I learned to see the difference between a boss and a botanist

Liam Shaw February 17, 2025

In the sclerosis of corporate hierarchies, middle management often finds itself caricatured as a relic of bygone organizational structures, scar tissue left behind by the Peter Principle. From the bumbling inefficiency of Office Space’s Lumbergh to the clichéd “paper-pusher,” middle managers have been the subject of fascination, frustration, and ridicule. But these stereotypes belie a deeper truth: middle managers, especially in knowledge work are essential stewards of an organizations most important and undervalued resource: the team.

From my experience as an Agile coach, I have seen firsthand how strong middle management can transform teams. When managers embrace a servant-leadership mindset — prioritizing the needs of their teams over the impulse to dictate outcomes — their teams flourish. Psychological safety increases, innovation thrives, and organizations become more resilient. Conversely, poor middle management can create bottlenecks, stifle creativity, and cause disengagement, ultimately leading to attrition and stagnation.

Organizations that undervalue middle managers risk missing out on their most significant leverage points for change. Leadership teams set the vision, but it is middle management that makes change stick. The bravest among them create feedback loops to share the “ground-truth” that leaders could never see. The real work of organizational transformation — whether it be cultural shifts, Agile adoption, or strategic pivots — lives in the day-to-day decisions and interactions managed at the working level.

When innovation originates at the team level and complexity outstrips top-down decision-making, the question isn’t whether middle management is relevant. The question is how the role can work to meet the evolving needs of organizations. This evolution requires us to think of middle management not as a title but as a discipline — a practice rooted in enabling others, fostering collaboration, and shaping environments where knowledge and creativity thrive.

From Title to Discipline: Middle Managers as Ecosystem Architects

For much of the 20th century, leadership was synonymous with expertise. Managers were the chess masters, moving pieces with precision, assumed to be the most knowledgeable in the room. But in today’s dynamic work environments, that model is can’t stay true. Innovation and knowledge creation now reside at the team level, where the people closest to the work hold the most up-to-date insights. After all, they are the ones creating and interpreting that knowledge. Through experimentation, incremental work, and reflection, teams refine their contributions in real time.

This shift leaves middle managers on the outside. If knowledge is created at the team level, managers simply can’t be the best informed. Their distance from the work makes this impossible. If they can no longer be the most informed decision-makers or technical experts, where can they add the most value?

This shift redefines the manager’s role from that of a directive authority to an “ecosystem architect,” someone who cultivates the conditions for innovation and collaboration to flourish. Middle managers are not here to dictate; they are here to build. They assemble diverse teams, foster the kinds of teams that dive deep together to uncover new ways to solve hard problems, and create an environment where collective intelligence can thrive.

As architects, middle managers can bridge the micro realities of team dynamics with the macro vision of organizational strategy. They are stewards of the processes, relationships, and cultures that transform individual effort into collective success. They don’t just enforce rules; they design systems that enable creativity and resilience. They act as gardeners of the ecosystem. Just as a gardener can’t will a plant to grow, they can control the environment and selection of plants.

Leadership as Gardening: Cultivating High-Performing Teams

The idea of leadership as gardening is perhaps best illustrated by U.S. General Stanley McChrystal, who famously redefined leadership during his time commanding the Joint Special Operations Task Force in Iraq. Faced with the fast-evolving landscapes of asymmetric guerrilla warfare, McChrystal realized that traditional, hierarchical command structures were insufficient. His leadership team had to evolve from a ground-war command style to one of distributed decision-making and adaptability.

McChrystal’s approach emphasized the need to cultivate environments where informed decisions could be made at all levels. Similarly, effective middle managers enable teams to adapt, innovate, and thrive in complex, dynamic environments — though surely with significantly less immediate stakes.

In organizations where ideas are the most valuable resource, middle managers act as gardeners nurturing thriving ecosystems. Middle managers, as gardeners of organizational ecosystems, take on a craft-oriented role, carefully shaping teams, processes, cultures, and norms to foster sustainable growth and innovation. Gardening is not passive; it requires intentional effort to balance competing priorities and ensure the ecosystem thrives as a whole.

Just as a gardener must weed out invasive plants that threaten the harmony of the garden, middle managers address behaviours or dynamics that disrupt team cohesion or effectiveness. They prune back certain aspects of processes or team interactions to create space for others to flourish, knowing that overgrowth in one area can overshadow critical contributions elsewhere. Similarly, they choose what to plant — assembling teams with diverse, complementary skills, perspectives, and strengths. This deliberate curation ensures that the soil of the team — its collective intelligence — is enriched and balanced, promoting long-term health and productivity.

Cultivating Psychological Safety

More important than anything else, middle managers must cultivate psychological safety within their teams. As Amy Edmondson emphasizes in her book Teaming, this involves fostering an environment where team members feel comfortable taking risks, voicing concerns, and learning from mistakes without fear of judgment or retribution.

This isn’t merely about avoiding blame or punishment but about shaping the very dynamics of how the team operates. Think of it like a game of strategic interaction. In a traditional, hierarchical setting, the default strategy for team members might be to prioritize self-preservation. Challenging a flawed idea or proposing a radical solution carries significant risks: potential embarrassment, social ostracization, or even career damage. The “payoff” for this risk-averse behavior is minimal — maintaining the status quo, avoiding conflict, and ensuring personal safety.

This dynamic mirrors Rousseau’s classic Stag Hunt. In this scenario, a group of hunters can either cooperate to hunt a large stag, providing a bountiful feast for all, or individually hunt for hares, guaranteeing a smaller but certain meal. If all hunters cooperate and focus on the stag, they achieve the optimal outcome. However, if even one hunter defects and pursues a hare, the stag will escape, leaving all with only meagre rabbit meat (which if hunters eat exclusively, they will perish from what is known a rabbit starvation).

In the absence of a shared understanding of the collective goal and a culture of trust and mutual support, individual self-interest often prevails. Team members, fearing the potential consequences of challenging the status quo or taking risks, opt for the safe option — pursuing individual goals or maintaining the status quo. This leads to a suboptimal outcome for the entire team, hindering innovation, stifling creativity, and ultimately limiting the team’s ability to achieve its full potential.

By cultivating a culture of psychological safety, middle managers can shift the game. They can raise the potential rewards for challenging the status quo while simultaneously lowering the negative consequences. This creates a virtuous cycle where:

  • Challenging ideas becomes the expected norm: Team members are encouraged to question assumptions, offer constructive criticism, and engage in open debate.
  • Collective intelligence flourishes: Diverse perspectives are valued, and the team leverages the combined knowledge and creativity of its members to achieve better outcomes.
  • Learning and innovation thrive: A culture of experimentation and learning from both successes and failures becomes embedded in the team’s DNA.

The Practical Value of Middle Management

By consciously shaping the social and interpersonal dynamics within their teams and cultivating a culture of trust and openness, middle managers can unlock the full potential of their teams and drive high performance.

Beyond assembling the right people and cultivating psychological safety, middle managers also:

  • Facilitate continuous learning: They create opportunities for reflection, experimentation, and knowledge sharing within and across teams. This might involve organizing workshops, team retrospectives, or knowledge-sharing sessions.
  • Build and maintain team networks: Middle managers help their teams connect with other teams, departments, and external stakeholders, fostering collaboration and access to diverse perspectives.
  • Adapt to changing circumstances: In today’s dynamic environment, middle managers must be agile and adaptable, supporting their teams as they navigate uncertainty and adjust to new challenges.

In this ecosystem, the manager’s role is not to dictate but to facilitate. They encourage cross-pollination of ideas by connecting teams across silos, ensuring that insights flow freely throughout the organization. They also act as guardians of focus, shielding their teams from unnecessary distractions while keeping them aligned with strategic priorities.

Investing in Teams: Lessons from Google

This is not simply a well-meaning or moral idea; it has real implications for organizational success. For example, tech companies like Google show us how valuable cohesive teams are in the knowledge economy. Their billion-dollar acquisitions aren’t just about technology — they’re about acquiring the brilliant teams behind that technology. When Google buys a firm like Raxium, it’s not just snapping up a product; it’s also bringing in the people who built it. A $1 billion purchase of a 50-person team? That’s $20 million per employee — proof that they value the people as much as, if not more than, the product itself.

Google’s approach to acquisitions reflects a mindset shift. They know that the expertise and creativity of a well-honed team are invaluable. Breezometer, for instance, wasn’t acquired solely for its air quality monitoring service. Even after shutting the product down, Google integrated the team’s knowledge into services like Google Maps. It’s not about the widget; it’s about the people behind it.

Conclusion: The Future of Middle Management

Middle management is not a relic of the past but an essential pillar of modern organizations. As knowledge work becomes more complex and dynamic, the role of middle managers must evolve accordingly. Their value lies not in control, but in cultivation — shaping the conditions that enable teams to thrive, adapt, and innovate.

The most effective middle managers are not bureaucratic bottlenecks but ecosystem architects, balancing autonomy with alignment and fostering environments where collective intelligence flourishes. They facilitate learning, remove obstacles, and build the connective tissue that allows organizations to harness the full potential of their people.

Far from being obsolete, middle management is more critical than ever. The organizations that recognize this — and invest in developing managers as enablers of high-performing teams — will be the ones that navigate complexity and change with the greatest agility. The future of work will not be shaped by rigid hierarchies, but by those who understand that leadership is less about directing and more about designing the right conditions for success. Hopefully with great mutual respect between themselves and agile coaches / scrum masters (the author wrote with no hidden personal agenda.)
