Diversity, Complexity and Disappointed Optimists

Liam Shaw June 5, 2023

In many creative activities the medium of execution is intractable. Lumber splits; paints smear; electrical circuits ring. These physical limitations of the medium constrain the ideas that may be expressed, and they also create unexpected difficulties in the implementation.

Implementation, then, takes time and sweat both because of the physical media and because of the inadequacies of the underlying ideas. We tend to blame the physical media for most of our implementation difficulties; for the media are not “ours” in the way the ideas are, and our pride colors our judgment.

- “The Mythical Man Month” — Fred Brooks, 1975

In the nearly half century since Fred Brooks articulated the idea of a “mythical man month,” the myth persists. He attributed the myth to optimism, that software development is a “modern sorcery [that] especially attracts those who believe in happy endings and fairy godmothers.”

The persistent mythology, that if we close our eyes, and think hard enough, that we can put together a perfectly tractable plan of what we want to build, how we can build it and who will need to pull it together, is an optimistic delusion. This optimism has real consequences, and I’ve witnessed language used by those concerned with diversity that reflects this as a foundation for some of their resistance to accepting diversity and inclusion not simply as a moral imperative, but as a benefit to improved performance.

In today’s ever changing and interconnected world, organizations are facing the challenge of complex work environments. More and more work is shifting from the complicated (difficult to fully grasp, but possible to learn over time) to the complex (highly interdependent and variable). Through this, I’ll make an effort to draw lines from complexity, management theory and diversity together into a mental model to add context to the relationship between diversity, inclusion, and collaboration in the context of complex work.

The Complicated and the Complex

As a starting point for moving further, I have to start with a few definitions.

  • We’ll take the idea of a “simple” problem as one in which there is a single optimal peak; the optimal weight of a shovel-full of coal to unload a cart in a 12 hour shift.
  • A complicated problem where there may be multiple “good” solutions and one “optimal” solution but once they are discovered through training and experience, they remain optimal. Imagine watch repair, there are multiple strategies for diagnosis and repair, but with enough experience a repair-person can find at least a personal (local) optimum.
  • And finally, the complex and chaotic environments of the dancing landscapes, in which optimums can only temporarily be discovered, a peak today may not be a peak tomorrow, an optimum may not be an optimum in a year, etc. An easy example would be financial markets, where any sufficiently good strategy, ceases to be a good strategy once other parties wise up to it (Adaptive Market Hypothesis).

Kicking and Screaming Into Complexity

When we traverse across that line, we have to discard many notions that worked in the predictable environment. Best practices become good practices, the very notion that a single practice can be best is a fools errand. Processes become anchors slowing down needed change. Staying still becomes as big a risk as changing, where adaptability is a dimension of organizational resilience (see BS 65000:2022 — Organizational Resilience).

All models are wrong, some are useful. — George Box, 1976

Amidst the ever-evolving landscape of modern work dynamics the model at hand may indeed falter in numerous aspects, no model will ever be perfect. However, at the heart of this idea lies a pivotal concept: a tipping point where the realm of work is no longer defined by the comfort of predictability, but by the challenges of unpredictability. A paradigm shift that encourages us to re-calibrate our approach. Embracing this metaphoric fault line between divergent landscapes is a challenge.

Sensing Complexity

It’s easy to see when it’s on a diagram but how can we sense when we’re working in a complex environment? There is no clear line between the two ideas in real life, with problems don’t come with a label. Some problems have puzzled mathematicians for years with no solution that seem easy but have no solution: the moving sofa problem (What is the largest area of a shape that can be maneuvered through a unit-width L-shaped corridor?) as an example, has no solution.

In Nassim Nicolas Taleb’s Antifragile he offers some indicators that a system is complex. That complex systems are characterized by intricate interdependencies and nonlinear responses. In these systems, doubling a factor does not result in a proportional doubling of the effect but rather an amplified or diminished impact. Simple cause-and-effect relationships do not hold, and understanding the functioning of individual parts alone is insufficient.

And so, we go full circle. The opening quote of this writing from the Mythical Man-Month, exemplifies this non-linearity. The conclusion that adding more developers to a late project tends to make them later, is a great indicator that software development, and likely all similarly intangible knowledge work, is complex, not simple or complicated. The rest of the writing will use software development and knowledge work generally, as marquee examples in comparison to other clearly simple or complicated types of work.

Diversity as a Tool in Knowledge Work

Knowledge workers are persons who understand their job better than their boss — Peter Drucker

If we are to accept that:

  • Knowledge work is complex and requires teams of skilled people working together
  • Complex work is unpredictable, interconnected and non-linear
  • The necessary tools required to or expertise to achieve positive results won’t be predictable (at least over a sufficient time space)

Then surely the natural response would be to seek not to build a team of “qualified” individuals who fit the same mould. Instead, an approach could be to optimize for the broadest germane skills and tools. Diversity of thought, over convergence to a mean.

The table above almost certainly sells the incredible skills of watchmaking short, but as a model, the key ideas hopefully hold up. The key concepts that we can tease out though are:

  • The outcome of work; the product, requires collaboration between team members with multiple skills where individual contribution alone cannot produce value
  • The skills needed to complete work usually require a breadth of skills and knowledge beyond a single individual’s capacity; a typical flow of requirements, design, implement, test and deploy may require all of the roles in the diagram to work together
  • The relationship between the team and the work isn’t predictable, some work may require a large amount of design, some may require extremely in-depth knowledge of development or knowledge of a new business domain.

Say this team had to balance two very big pieces of work in their system: a large security issue and a challenging high priority feature request. What could they do as a first step? Look at the new work and ask themselves, what they might be missing to address them, and seek out adding that to the team. It is only the team though who could assess this as it is based on their collective skills.

Let’s assume the team looks inward and decides they need to add a new member to address these two needs. It’s likely they come to a conclusion that the new member should not have the same background as themselves. Additionally, if they foresee that future work will require this same bringing in of new knowledge, then the optimal approach of who to bring in is to bring someone in who looks like the least like them with the most different perspectives and heuristics.

Cognitive diversity builds their capacity to address the problems of today and better position themselves to address problems in the future.

Sympathy for the Devil

When I imagine a worker spending all day shovelling coal, seeing their daily pay and seeing a smaller pile of coal next to theirs done by another worker paid the same it doesn’t feel like a stretch to imagine the discontentment they may feel. The same might be felt by a craftsman who built a watch (or more watches) of higher quality than a peer who also was paid the same. In these environments, perhaps some discontentment that a “diversity hire” could be brought in may feel a little more justified.

However, when looking at knowledge work, where complexity is the norm, how can we justify that same sentiment? I’d argue that we simply can’t. The volatility, uncertainty, complexity, and ambiguity of the work environment ought to compel teams to add skills different from their own as a route to making themselves and their teams better. Holding onto notions of strict “qualifications” serve to limit the pool of perspectives and tools that teams may have access to. Certainly, there’s a need to retain germane skills but when work requires increasing breadth of knowledge can we reasonably expect to address new challenges by bringing in people who look, act and work just like us?
